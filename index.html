Understanding Child Abuse

Child abuse is any action by another person that causes significant harm to a child. It can be physical, sexual, emotional, or neglect. Recognizing the signs and knowing how to respond can save a child's life.

Remember: If you suspect a child is being abused, it's better to report and be wrong than to stay silent. You could save a child's life.

Recognizing the Signs

Being aware of these warning signs can help you identify when a child may need help.

Physical Abuse
  • Unexplained bruises, burns, or injuries
  • Frequent injuries or patterns of injury
  • Fear of going home or being with certain people
Emotional Abuse
  • Low self-esteem, depression, excessive fear or anxiety
  • Difficulty forming relationships
  • Delayed emotional development
Sexual Abuse
  • Inappropriate sexual knowledge or behavior
  • Fear of certain people or places
  • Difficulty walking or sitting
Neglect
  • Poor hygiene or inappropriate clothing
  • Frequent absence from school
  • Lack of medical or dental care
Important: The presence of one sign doesn't necessarily mean abuse is occurring. However, if you notice multiple signs or patterns, it's important to take action.

Prevention Starts With You

Simple steps everyone can take to create a safer environment for children.

Educate Children

Teach children about body safety, appropriate touch, and that they have the right to say no.

Open Communication

Create an environment where children feel safe to talk about their feelings and experiences.

Monitor Relationships

Know who spends time with your children and be aware of any concerning behavior.

Build Support Networks

Connect with other parents, teachers, and community members to create a protective environment.

Trust Your Instincts

If something feels wrong, it probably is. Don't ignore warning signs or gut feelings.

Stay Informed

Learn about child development, abuse prevention, and local resources available to help.

Remember: Prevention is a Community Effort

Creating a safe environment for children requires everyone's involvement. By staying informed, vigilant, and supportive, we can work together to prevent child abuse and protect our most vulnerable.
